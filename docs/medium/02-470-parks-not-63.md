## “National park” in conversation vs. on the sign

When friends say “let’s do a national park trip,” they usually mean the flagship parks—mountains, canyons, famous campgrounds.

The Park Service uses **designations** on the sign at the gate. Same agency, different labels:

| What it’s called | What it’s often like |
|------------------|----------------------|
| **National Park** | Big landscapes—hiking, wildlife, classic road-trip parks |
| **National Monument** | Smaller but stunning—ruins, fossils, dramatic rock |
| **National Historic Site / Historical Park** | Homes, forts, battlefields, city walks with rangers |
| **National Memorial** | Places built to remember people or events |
| **National Seashore / Lakeshore** | Beaches, dunes, boating, coast trips |
| **National Recreation Area** | Lakes and reservoirs—swimming, camping, boats |
| **National Preserve** | Wild country, sometimes with hunting or fishing allowed |
| **Other NPS units** | Parkways, trails, and more |

**The simple version:** “National park” in everyday talk is not the same as “everything the Park Service takes care of.” Hundreds of other sites are real NPS destinations—with visitor centers, ranger programs, and rules worth checking before you go.

**TrailVerse includes all of those designations**—not a trimmed list of only the famous parks.

Fall color doesn’t have to mean only Acadia or Shenandoah. Historical parks, memorials, and lakeshores can give you color and quieter crowds.

**AllTrails** is great when you already know *which park* and *which trail* you’re hiking—GPS on the path, crowdsourced trail notes.

If you only ever open a trail app, you’ll think the whole system is “hikes only.” It isn’t.

## Plan a trip on TrailVerse: famous parks first, then the full system

You don’t have to ignore Yellowstone. Here’s a workflow that matches how the site is laid out:

1. **Shortlist the famous parks** — On **Explore**, leave **National Parks Only** on. Filter by state, pick three to five names you care about.  

2. **Widen the net** — Toggle **National Parks Only** off, or open **Discover** and choose a **type** (Monument, Seashore, Historic Site) or **activity** (stargazing, paddling, wildlife). Add anything interesting on your route.  

3. **Check today’s reality** — Open each finalist on TrailVerse → **Alerts** and **weather**. For timed entry and camps, use **Recreation.gov**; for official safety wording, use **NPS.gov**.  

4. **Choose between finalists** — Use **Compare** for two to four options, then **Trailie** with your dates, group, and pace (“easy,” “photo-focused,” “one big hike per day”).  

5. **Hit the trail** — TrailVerse for the trip; your favorite trail app for the single hike.

Use **TrailVerse** to explore and plan across the full system; use [**Recreation.gov**](https://www.recreation.gov) when you’re ready to book campgrounds, timed entry, and permits. The point is simple: don’t plan a “national park trip” while ignoring **400+ other NPS places** you could have reached in the same drive.
