# Tone Standards

Apply these rules to both articles. Non-negotiable.

Benchmarks: The Points Guy (Article 1) and PhotoPills (Article 2).

---

## Universal Rules (Both Articles)

- **Max 4 sentences per paragraph. Hard limit.** Split any paragraph that exceeds this.
- Target 50–80 words per paragraph (Article 1); 40–70 words (Article 2)
- One idea per paragraph — never combine two thoughts
- Mix short punchy sentences with slightly longer context sentences
- Contractions always: "you'll", "it's", "don't", "won't"
- Write TO the reader ("you"), never AT them ("visitors should")
- Active voice always — never passive
- Lead every section with the most useful information first — never with framing or context
- **Forbidden everywhere:** "approximately", "it is worth noting", "one might",
  "roughly speaking", "in essence"

---

## Article 1 Tone — The Points Guy

Model: knowledgeable friend texting advice the night before your trip.

- Practical info leads, color/storytelling follows
- No literary cleverness that slows a scanning reader
- Bold the single most important takeaway per section
- **Forbidden phrases:** "The honest answer is…" / "And Why It Changes Everything" /
  "It is worth noting" / "one might observe"

**Examples:**
| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| "Canyonlands rewards visitors who understand what they're walking into." | "Most people underestimate Canyonlands. Here's what to know." |
| "The honest answer is that the best time to visit depends entirely on what you're optimizing for." | "The best time depends on what you want. Here's the breakdown." |
| "Geography of consequence" / "rewards visitors who understand" / "hard to photograph but more profound" | Cut all of it — say the useful thing directly |

## Article 2 Tone — PhotoPills

Model: enthusiastic photographer peer who's shot this location and wants you to get the shot.

- ACTION before explanation — tell them WHAT to do first, WHY in one follow-up sentence
- Enthusiasm is appropriate — this is a photographer talking to a photographer
- **Forbidden in body prose** (data bullets only, never in running text):
  - SQM numbers (21.85 mag/arcsec²)
  - Magnitude numbers (+4.6, +5.1)
  - Celestial mechanics ("declination of −29°", "transits due south at approximately 23°")
  - Sensor specs ("pixel pitch", "BSI-CMOS", "EXPEED")
- Plain English required for every technical term:
  - "visible with the naked eye" — not "+4.6 magnitude"
  - "the sky is dark enough to cast shadows" — not "SQM 22.02"
- **Tips lists:** Bold label + 1–2 sentences max. Never paragraph-length tips.
- **Deep sky objects:** Pick 2–3 max. Describe what they look like visually, not
  scientifically. No magnitude numbers. No cataloguing.
- **Gear section:** Lead with the most critical practical item as a bold bullet.
- **Ranger programs:** Cut tourism-brochure language. Say what it is, link to schedule.

**Examples:**
| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| "The galactic core sits at a declination of roughly −29°, which at Canyonlands' latitude of 38°N means it rises in the southeast, transits due south at a maximum altitude of approximately 23°…" | "The Milky Way core rises in the southeast, swings through due south, and sets in the southwest. At Canyonlands' latitude it peaks at about 23° above the horizon — not high, so pick a location with a clear southern view." |
| "M8 Lagoon Nebula (magnitude +4.6) appears as a soft misty patch" | "The Lagoon Nebula is visible with the naked eye here — looks like a soft smudge just above the Sagittarius Teapot." |
| "Set ISO 3200 to start. At Bortle Class 2, you don't need to push ISO to extremes because the sky itself is providing signal." | "Set ISO 3200 to start. The sky here is so dark you might not even need 6400." |
