# Zion Astrophotography Guide: Bortle Class, Milky Way Calendar & Nikon Z6II Settings (2026)

Stand on the east rim after astronomical twilight and the Milky Way can look like smoke pouring over Navajo sandstone — that's what a **zion astrophotography guide** should prepare you for. Zion holds **Bortle Class 3–4** skies (canyon floor toward 4, Kolob and high rim toward 3) and is certified as an [International Dark Sky Park](https://darksky.org/places/zion-national-park-dark-sky-park/). You won't get Bryce-level darkness, but you **will** get canyon walls as foregrounds Bryce can't match.

> 📋 **For trail info, seasonal strategy, and trip logistics, see the [Zion Complete Guide](/blog/zion-national-park-the-complete-2026-visitor-guide). This article is purely about the night sky.**

## Dark Sky Data

**What is Zion's Bortle class?** Zion National Park rates **Bortle 3–4** depending on elevation — roughly **Bortle 4 on the canyon floor** and **Bortle 3 on Kolob Canyons and east rim pullouts**, based on [LightCast's Zion astro analysis](https://lightcastsuite.com/blog/zion-astrophotography). The park is an **International Dark Sky Park** certified by [DarkSky International](https://darksky.org/places/zion-national-park-dark-sky-park/).

- **Bortle Class:** 3–4 (rim and Kolob toward 3; main canyon floor toward 4)
- **Sky Quality:** Strong naked-eye Milky Way from open horizons; not "shadow-casting dark" like Bortle 2 sites
- **DarkSky Certification:** International Dark Sky Park
- **Darkest practical sites:** Kolob Canyons viewpoints, east rim above tunnel
- **Light pollution direction:** **Springdale glow to the south** — compose with the canyon between you and town when you can

**Plain English:** From a rim pullout you'll see the Milky Way band clearly on a moonless night. The core won't look as electric as Bryce, but sandstone silhouettes at 2,000 feet of relief make up for it. On the canyon floor, walls block the horizon early — great for intimacy, harder for ultra-wide core panoramas.

## Milky Way Visibility Calendar

At **37°N**, the galactic core rises in the **southeast**, crosses **due south** at modest altitude (roughly low-20s degrees above the horizon), and sets **southwest**. That low arc means you need **open southern sky** — rim roads and Kolob, not deep inside the narrows.

Astronomical darkness runs about **90–105 minutes after sunset**. In summer that's often **10:00–10:30 p.m.**; in May and September closer to **9:30 p.m.**

**Light geometry:** Face **south or southeast** from east rim and Kolob. Avoid pointing straight at **Springdale** — warm sky glow will bloom in long exposures.

## Best Shooting Locations

**1. Canyon Overlook / east rim corridor**

Park at the Canyon Overlook trail area or designated east-side pullouts (confirm current NPS night parking rules before you go). You're above the tunnel with **open southern pockets** and massive wall silhouettes.

Walk is short by day; at night use a **red headlamp** and stay on paved/pullout surfaces. Best **May–September** when the core spans across the south sky. Springdale's dome sits low to the south — shoot slightly east of south to keep glow off the core.

Foreground options: layered sandstone ridges, occasional pine silhouettes, no light painting on rock (NPS dark-sky ethics).

**2. Kolob Canyons section**

Drive the Kolob road for **cleaner Bortle 3** skies with less Springdale scatter. Fewer people, more plateau openness, different rock palette than main canyon.

You'll trade shuttle convenience for a **40+ minute drive** from Springdale — factor that into last-light timing. Summer nights are mild; spring/fall bring wind. Foreground: high desert mesas and distant peaks — excellent for 14mm Milky Way panoramas.

**3. Human History Museum / Pa'rus Trail vicinity**

Lower elevation and more scattered light, but this is where [NPS stargazing programs](https://www.nps.gov/thingstodo/stargazing-in-zion.htm) often run — useful for scouting safe parking and ranger-led orientation.

Use this zone when you want **people-scale foregrounds** (trees, river corridor) rather than cliff drama. Pair a program night for scouting, return solo on new moon for real shooting.

## Nikon Z6II Settings

The Z6II handles high-ISO noise well enough that you don't have to torture shadows at Bortle 3–4. **500-rule at 14mm:** 500 ÷ 14 ≈ **35 seconds max** before stars trail — start at **20–25 seconds** and zoom review at 10×.

| Scenario | ISO | Aperture | Shutter | White Balance | Lens |
|----------|-----|----------|---------|---------------|------|
| Milky Way Wide Field | 3200–6400 | f/4 | 20–25s | 4000K | NIKKOR Z 14-30mm f/4 S @ 14mm |
| Star Trails (interval stack) | 800–1600 | f/4 | 120–180s/frame | 4000K fixed | NIKKOR Z 14-30mm f/4 S |
| Moon + Landscape | 400–800 | f/5.6–8 | 1/4–2s | Daylight | NIKKOR Z 24-70mm f/2.8 S |

**Long Exposure NR: OFF** — the camera will double your exposure time and erase meteors between frames.

**IBIS/VR: OFF on tripod** — stabilization can blur long exposures on a solid tripod.

**Focus:** Manual, magnify live view 10× on a bright star or distant porch light on the rim — don't trust infinity marks at f/4.

**Release:** 2-second self-timer or wired remote — even tripod collar movement matters on 25s shots.

**Foreground blending:** Shoot one sky frame (20–25s, ISO 3200) and one dim ground frame (2–4 minutes, ISO 800, same WB) — blend in Lightroom or Photoshop; canyon walls need separation from pure black.

**NPS policy:** No light painting on rock formations — let moon phase or starlight carry foreground detail.

## What to Photograph (Deep Sky Objects)

**Milky Way core** — the bright band through Sagittarius and the Teapot asterism; this is the hero target June–August from rim locations.

**Lagoon Nebula area** — looks like a soft smudge near the Teapot; visible naked eye on the darkest Kolob nights.

**Andromeda Galaxy** — autumn shoulder season from east rim before winter cold; small oval smear, best with a tracker or stacked exposures if you want detail beyond a wide field.

Keep object count low. Zion sells **landscape astro**, not deep-sky catalog hunting.

## Ranger Programs & Astronomy Events

Zion lists [stargazing and astronomy activities](https://www.nps.gov/thingstodo/stargazing-in-zion.htm) seasonally — check the park calendar for ranger-led night sky events near the Human History Museum area. Programs are great for **first-night orientation**; serious shooters still want a **new-moon rim session** on their own schedule.

If programs are paused, Bryce Canyon and Cedar Breaks (both darker Bortle) are common add-on astro nights on Utah Mighty Five loops — but Zion's canyon foregrounds stay unique.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What Bortle class is Zion National Park?**
Zion is generally **Bortle 3–4** — darker on the east rim and Kolob Canyons, brighter on the canyon floor near Springdale's light dome. It's certified as an International Dark Sky Park.

**What is the best month for Milky Way photography at Zion?**
**July 2026** around the **July 14 new moon** is the strongest combo of core height, warm nights, and park access. June and August are close seconds; avoid full-moon weeks unless you're shooting moonlit canyon walls.

**What Nikon Z6II settings should I use at Zion?**
Start at **14mm, f/4, 20–25s, ISO 3200**, white balance **4000K**, with Long Exposure NR off. Drop ISO to 1600–800 if you stack or use a tracker at Kolob.

**Where is the best Milky Way spot in Zion?**
**Canyon Overlook / east rim** for canyon silhouettes with southern sky; **Kolob Canyons** for the darkest sky in the park. Avoid deep in the Narrows at night — walls block the core and flash-flood risk is not worth it.

**Can you see the Milky Way from Zion Canyon floor?**
Sometimes, but walls cut the horizon and Springdale glow is stronger. You'll get better images from **rim or Kolob** pullouts with open south/southeast views.
